Signs Your Belt Is on Borrowed Time

  • Squealing or chirping at startup or when turning
  • Visible cracks, glazing, or fraying on the belt surface
  • A battery light flickering on and off
  • Stiff power steering or an AC that cuts in and out
  • More than 60,000–100,000 miles on the original belt

Your serpentine belt drives the alternator, power steering, water pump, and AC. When it snaps, you lose all of them at once — usually at the worst possible moment. Replacement is cheap insurance.

Belt Replacement FAQs

What's the difference between a serpentine belt and a timing belt?

The serpentine belt runs your accessories (alternator, AC, power steering) and is visible on the front of the engine. The timing belt is hidden inside covers and keeps the engine's valves synchronized — if it fails on many engines, the result is catastrophic internal damage.

What happens if my serpentine belt breaks while driving?

You'll lose power steering, the alternator stops charging, and on many vehicles the water pump stops — meaning the engine can overheat within minutes. Pull over safely and call for a tow rather than trying to limp it home.

When should the timing belt be replaced?

Most manufacturers specify 60,000–105,000 miles. If you bought the vehicle used and can't confirm it was done, it's safest to replace it — the belt costs far less than the engine it protects.
