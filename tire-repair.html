Tire Problems We Fix Fast

  • Flat tires and slow leaks from nails, screws, and road debris
  • Tires that lose air every week
  • Vibration or shaking at highway speed, usually a balance issue
  • Uneven wear on the inside or outside edges
  • TPMS (tire pressure) warning lights

Most punctures in the tread are repairable with a proper patch-plug from the inside. That's a permanent, safe repair that costs a fraction of a new tire. We'll tell you honestly when a tire can be saved and when it can't.

Columbia County Roads Are Hard on Tires

Between construction traffic on Highway 30, gravel driveways and logging roads, and the debris that washes onto pavement every wet season, tires around here pick up more punctures than the national average. And the shoulder of a rural highway is a bad place to discover it. Most of the flats we see come in with a screw or roofing nail square in the tread, which is the best-case puncture: caught early, it's a permanent repair instead of a new tire.

The other local pattern is corrosion. Wheels that live through Oregon winters build up oxidation where the tire bead seats, and that causes slow leaks with no puncture at all. Plenty of drivers replace a perfectly good tire chasing a leak that was actually the rim. We check the bead seat, the valve stem, and the wheel itself before we blame the tire, because the fix for a corroded bead seat costs far less than rubber you didn't need.

What Does a Tire Repair Cost?

A proper patch-plug repair is one of the cheapest fixes in the shop. It runs a small fraction of what a single new tire costs, and it takes about half an hour. What changes the price is what we find. A repairable tread puncture is the simple case, while a sidewall injury, a tire worn below safe tread depth, or damage from being driven flat means replacement is the only safe option.

Either way, you'll know before we start. We pull the tire, show you what we found, and give you a firm price for the repair. If the tire can't be saved, you'll get an honest comparison of replacement options instead. Nobody here earns a commission for selling you tires, so the recommendation you get is the one we'd give a neighbor.

Tire Repair FAQs

Is a patch or a plug better?

The right answer is both: an internal patch-plug combination seals the liner and fills the puncture channel. An outside-only plug is a roadside stopgap, not a permanent repair.

Can a sidewall puncture be repaired?

No. Punctures in the sidewall or outer shoulder can't be safely repaired, because that area flexes constantly. In that case the tire needs replacement.

How long does a tire repair take?

Usually 20–30 minutes once we have the vehicle in a bay. Call ahead and we'll get you in and out fast.

Is it safe to keep driving on a slow leak?

Not for long. A tire that's low on air flexes more, builds heat, and wears its shoulders. And a slow leak can become a fast one on the highway. Topping it off every week doesn't fix the problem; it just delays a repair that takes half an hour.

Why does my tire pressure light come on in cold weather?

Tire pressure drops about 1 PSI for every 10-degree drop in temperature, so the first cold snap of fall lights up TPMS warnings all over Columbia County. If the light stays on after the tires are set to spec, a sensor or a real leak is the cause, and we can check both.

How often should tires be rotated?

Every 5,000–7,500 miles for most vehicles, which is roughly every other oil change. Front tires wear faster because they steer and carry the engine's weight; rotation spreads that wear so all four tires retire together instead of two at a time.

Do you repair truck and trailer tires?

Yes. Pickups, work trucks, and most trailer tires are no problem. If you're not sure whether we can handle your setup, call and describe it, and we'll give you a straight answer before you drive in.
